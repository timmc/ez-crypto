A good passphrase is important

A passphrase is just a password on steroids. The password you use for some random website that you don't care about very much might look like paramount5 or ^not&secure — something very simple with a couple of numbers or punctuation symbols carelessly tossed in. A well-written cracking program could guess those in a few minutes! A passphrase, on the other hand, is expected to be strong. It's best to generate them randomly and then figure out mnemonics that will help you remember them. Good passphrases look like 3AhWv4'Q#5h2IM1Gt or felnroth LKKrl pi[toacl or xrategic(level of the Quadrenni9l De%ense Review (QDR) 2006 goals.

The more like normal text they look, the longer they should be.

How am I supposed to memorize that?

In the general case, the more secure a passphrase is against attack, the harder it will be to memorize. This is because advanced passphrase-cracking programs are written to prioritize guesses that people would find memorable. There are two tricks I will recommend to combat this unfortunate tension:

Write it down

While passphrases on paper are less secure if someone is targeting you (e.g. stalker, police), they're fine as a temporary measure until you succeed in memorizing the passphrase — and perfectly fine if you're only worried about digital snooping. Tips for this strategy:

  • When you have it memorized, store the passphrase somewhere safe. This might be your safe deposit box or in a jar buried in the woods.
  • Don't write down what it's for on the piece of paper, unless you're OK with the finder knowing. This may sound obvious, but it's worth mentioning.

Take an odd sentence and then distort it

"Odd" here just means a sentence that most people would find opaque, hard to understand, or technical. Use uncommon words like gneiss, proprioceptive, or infundibulum. Using another language is great, but don't pick a well-known phrase. This is a good way of generating something that an automated password cracker is unlikely to guess, since they prioritize more common words.

Then, distort the sentence by inserting, changing, or deleting random characters at random locations. (It's best to rely on a random number generator for this, since humans are notoriously bad at being unpredictable.) You'll be able to remember these few alterations much more effectively than a whole sentence full of random characters. The result is a pretty effective passphrase!
